Core Principles
PRINCIPLE 1
SWIPE
‹
›
Decentralization
No single authority controls the network. Power is distributed across thousands of nodes globally, making blockchain resistant to censorship, single points of failure, and corrupt control. Unlike traditional banks, no one entity owns the ledger.
No Central Authority P2P Network Censorship Resistant
15,000+ full nodes globally
PRINCIPLE 2
SWIPE
‹
›
Immutability
Once data is written to a block and confirmed, it is cryptographically sealed forever. Altering even one character would invalidate that block's hash and every block after it — making tampering computationally and economically impossible.
Cryptographic Hash Sealed Blocks Tamper-Proof
99.99% data integrity rate
PRINCIPLE 3
SWIPE
‹
›
Transparency
Every transaction is publicly visible on the ledger. Anyone can audit the full history of any wallet or smart contract — eliminating hidden fees, back-door deals, and accounting fraud at an institutional level.
Public Ledger Auditable Full History
Every tx visible to anyone
PRINCIPLE 4
SWIPE
‹
›
Security
Military-grade SHA-256 encryption protects every transaction. A 51% attack on Bitcoin would require more computing power than the world's top 500 supercomputers combined — making attacks virtually impossible and economically irrational.
SHA-256 51% Attack Proof Fortress Grade
$0 in protocol-level hacks ever

›
Trustless System
You don't need to trust a bank, government, or company — only the math. Smart contracts execute automatically when conditions are met. No middlemen, no delays, no possibility of human interference, corruption, or error.
No Middlemen Code is Law Auto-Execute
Smart contracts since 2015
How It Works — Step by Step
STEP 1
SWIPE
‹
›
Transaction Initiated
A user creates a transaction — sending cryptocurrency, executing a smart contract, storing data, or transferring an NFT. The transaction is digitally signed with the sender's private key using elliptic curve cryptography, proving ownership.
Private Key Sign Digital Signature ECDSA
Triggered by the user
STEP 2
SWIPE
‹
›
Network Broadcast
The signed transaction is broadcast to all nodes in the peer-to-peer network simultaneously. Within milliseconds, thousands of nodes across the globe receive and acknowledge the pending transaction in their mempool queue.
P2P Broadcast Mempool Milliseconds
Milliseconds to propagate
STEP 3
SWIPE
‹
›
Validation
Nodes validate the transaction — checking the digital signature, ensuring no double-spend, verifying sufficient balance. Miners or validators compete to add the transaction to the next block using their respective consensus rules.
Signature Check Balance Verified No Double Spend
Consensus mechanism runs
STEP 4
SWIPE
‹
›
Block Formation
Valid transactions are bundled into a new block containing: a Merkle root of all transactions, the previous block's hash, a timestamp, and a nonce. The block header links it cryptographically to every block before it.
Merkle Tree Prev Hash Timestamp
~2,000 txs per Bitcoin block
STEP 5
SWIPE
‹
›
Chain Addition & Finality
The winning miner broadcasts the completed block. All nodes verify and add it to their chain. After 6 confirmations on Bitcoin (~60 minutes), the transaction is considered irreversible and permanently sealed in the blockchain's history forever.
Confirmations Permanent Finality
~60 min for full finality

Consensus Mechanisms
CONSENSUS
SWIPE
‹
›
Proof of Work (PoW)
Miners compete to solve a complex mathematical puzzle. The first to solve it earns the right to add the next block and receives a block reward. This process consumes enormous energy but provides unmatched security through economic deterrence.
Bitcoin Energy Intensive Most Secure
Used by: Bitcoin, Litecoin
CONSENSUS
SWIPE
‹
›
Proof of Stake (PoS)
Validators lock up (stake) their cryptocurrency as collateral. They are chosen to validate blocks proportional to their stake. Malicious behavior results in slashing. Uses 99.9% less energy than PoW — used by Ethereum since The Merge in 2022.
Ethereum Eco-Friendly Staking Rewards
Used by: ETH, Cardano, Polkadot
CONSENSUS
SWIPE
‹
›
Delegated PoS (DPoS)
Token holders vote for elected delegates who validate transactions. Creates a more democratic system with faster speeds. Delegates risk losing their elected position if they act dishonestly — achieving high performance through social accountability.
Voting System Ultra Fast Democratic
Used by: EOS, TRON, STEEM
CONSENSUS
SWIPE
‹
›
Byzantine Fault Tolerance
BFT systems reach consensus even when some nodes are faulty or malicious. Named after the Byzantine Generals Problem. Used in enterprise blockchains where instant finality and high performance are prioritized over maximum decentralization.
Enterprise Grade Instant Finality High TPS
Used by: Hyperledger, Tendermint

Key Components
COMPONENT
SWIPE
‹
›
Block Structure
Each block holds: a header (hash, previous hash, timestamp, merkle root, nonce) and the transaction data body. The header cryptographically links it to the previous block. Bitcoin blocks are capped at 1–4MB, containing ~2,000 transactions.
Block Header Merkle Root Nonce
COMPONENT
SWIPE
‹
›
Cryptographic Hash
SHA-256 converts any input into a fixed 256-bit output. Change one character and the entire hash changes completely. Impossible to reverse-engineer. This is what makes the blockchain tamper-evident — any change is instantly detectable.
SHA-256 Deterministic Irreversible
COMPONENT
SWIPE
‹
›
Merkle Tree
A binary tree where every transaction is hashed, then pairs of hashes are hashed together, up to a single Merkle Root. Allows verification that any transaction exists in a block without downloading the entire blockchain — enabling lightweight SPV wallets.
Binary Tree SPV Proof Efficient Verify
COMPONENT
SWIPE
‹
›
Smart Contracts
Self-executing programs stored on-chain. Written in Solidity (Ethereum), Rust (Solana), or Plutus (Cardano). They execute automatically when triggered — no lawyers, no banks, no enforcement needed. The foundation of DeFi, NFTs, and DAOs.
Auto-Execute Solidity/Rust No Middlemen
COMPONENT
SWIPE
‹
›
Public/Private Keys
Based on elliptic curve cryptography (secp256k1). Your public key is your address — share freely. Your private key signs transactions — never share. Losing your private key means losing your funds permanently. "Not your keys, not your coins."
Public Address Private Signing Non-Custodial
COMPONENT
SWIPE
‹
›
Network Nodes
Full nodes store the entire blockchain (500GB+ for Bitcoin) and independently verify every transaction. Light nodes store only headers. Mining nodes create new blocks. More nodes = greater decentralization, security, and censorship resistance.
Full Node Light Node Miner Node

Zero-Knowledge Proofs
ZK BASICS
SWIPE
‹
›
What Is a ZK Proof?
A Zero-Knowledge Proof lets Party A prove to Party B that they know a secret — without revealing the secret itself. Classic example: prove you know a password without typing it. On blockchain, ZK proofs allow transaction validation without exposing sender, receiver, or amount — a cryptographic breakthrough from the 1980s now powering Web3.
No Data Revealed Math Verified Privacy Tech
ZK-ROLLUPS
SWIPE
‹
›
ZK-Rollups & Scaling
ZK-Rollups bundle thousands of off-chain transactions into a single validity proof posted on Ethereum mainnet. zkSync Era processes 2,000+ TPS with sub-cent fees while inheriting Ethereum's full security. StarkNet uses STARKs (Scalable Transparent ARguments of Knowledge) to achieve massive throughput with no trusted setup required.
Batch Processing 2000+ TPS Sub-cent Fees

Zcash (ZEC) uses zk-SNARKs for optional private "shielded" transactions. Both are banned by several exchanges due to regulatory pressure.

Blockchain Layers: L0 to L3
LAYER 0
SWIPE
‹
›
Layer 0 — The Infrastructure
Layer 0 is the underlying network and hardware that blockchains run on. Polkadot's relay chain and Cosmos's Inter-Blockchain Communication (IBC) protocol are L0 networks that allow entirely separate blockchains to communicate and share security. Think of L0 as the internet — L1s are websites built on top of it.
Cross-Chain IBC Polkadot Relay Shared Security
LAYER 1
SWIPE
‹
›
Layer 1 — Base Blockchains
Layer 1s are the base-layer blockchains with their own validators, consensus, and security: Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana, Avalanche, BNB Chain, Cardano, and Aptos. They are the settlement layer — all L2 activity ultimately posts proofs back to an L1. Ethereum L1 handles ~15 TPS but secures $200B+ in value.
Base Settlement Self-Sovereign Consensus Layer
LAYER 2
SWIPE
‹
›
Layer 2 — Scaling Solutions
L2s execute transactions off-chain and post compressed proofs on L1. Arbitrum and Optimism use Optimistic Rollups (7-day challenge window). Polygon zkEVM, zkSync, and Starknet use ZK-Rollups (instant finality). The Lightning Network is Bitcoin's L2 — enabling 1M+ TPS micropayments for near-zero fees.
100K+ TPS <$0.01 Fees L1 Security
LAYER 3
SWIPE
‹
›
Layer 3 — Application Layer
Layer 3 sits on top of L2s and provides application-specific chains or hyper-scaling environments. Arbitrum Orbit and Optimism's OP Stack let developers launch customised L3 chains with their own gas tokens and rules. Telegram's TON blockchain essentially operates as an L3 ecosystem with 900M+ potential users.
App-Specific Orbit/OP Stack TON Ecosystem

How NFTs Actually Work
An NFT (Non-Fungible Token) is an ERC-721 or ERC-1155 smart contract on Ethereum (or Solana's SPL standard). The NFT itself stores: owner address, token ID, and a metadata URI pointing to IPFS or Arweave. The image is NOT stored on-chain — just the pointer. This means if the hosting goes down, your NFT image disappears, though ownership proof remains forever.

Hot Wallets
Hot wallets are internet-connected software wallets: MetaMask (40M+ users), Phantom (Solana), Trust Wallet, Coinbase Wallet. They are browser extensions or mobile apps that store private keys locally. Convenient for DeFi interaction but vulnerable to malware, phishing, and browser exploits. ‹

Cold / Hardware Wallets
Hardware wallets (Ledger, Trezor) store private keys on an air-gapped chip that never touches the internet. Transaction signing happens on the device — even if your PC is hacked, funds stay safe. Ledger Nano X supports 5,500+ coins. Best practice: $100 Ledger protects $100,000 in crypto. Always buy directly from manufacturer — never second-hand.
Air-Gapped Keys Hack-Proof Ledger/Trezor
SEED PHRASES

‹

Seed Phrases: 12 Words = Everything
A BIP-39 seed phrase is 12-24 words that can fully restore any wallet. It is the master key to all accounts derived from it. Write it on paper — never digitally. Never photograph it. Never type it into any website. Store in 2 locations. A metal backup plate survives fire and flooding. Over 1 million BTC is lost because owners lost their seed phrases.
BIP-39 Standard Never Digital Metal Backup

ASIC vs GPU Mining
ASICs (Application-Specific Integrated Circuits) are chips built solely for mining one algorithm. Bitmain's Antminer S21 achieves 200 TH/s at 3550W. A single ASIC outperforms 10,000 CPUs at Bitcoin's SHA-256. GPU mining thrives on ASIC-resistant algorithms (Ethereum used Ethash before the Merge, Ravencoin uses KawPow). Post-Merge, most GPUs became unprofitable overnight.

Essential Crypto Glossary
Key Terms Every Crypto User Must Know
From HODL to zkEVM — the most important terminology in blockchain
HODL Hold On for Dear Life. Originated from a 2013 Bitcoin Forum typo. Strategy of holding crypto through volatility rather than trading. Historically outperforms active trading for most retail investors.
DYOR Do Your Own Research. Never invest based solely on social media, influencers, or anonymous tips. Read whitepapers, audit reports, tokenomics, and team backgrounds before investing.
GAS FEES The computational cost paid to miners/validators to process transactions on a blockchain. Ethereum gas is priced in Gwei (1 billionth of 1 ETH). During NFT mints or DeFi frenzies, gas can spike to $200+ per transaction.
MEMPOOL Memory Pool. A staging area where unconfirmed transactions wait before being included in a block. During high network congestion, thousands of transactions queue. Higher-fee transactions get priority. MEV (Maximal Extractable Value) bots front-run transactions from the mempool.
SLIPPAGE The difference between the expected price of a trade and the actual execution price. In DEXs with low liquidity, large trades move the price significantly. Always set slippage tolerance carefully — too low fails the TX, too high enables sandwich attacks from MEV bots.
TVL Total Value Locked. The dollar value of crypto assets deposited in a DeFi protocol. Used as a key metric to measure protocol adoption and health. DeFi's total TVL peaked at $180B in Nov 2021. DefiLlama tracks TVL for thousands of protocols in real-time.
WHALE An entity holding enough crypto to significantly move market prices. In Bitcoin, wallets holding 1,000+ BTC qualify. Whale movements tracked on-chain via Whale Alert. A single whale transaction can shift small-cap token prices 20-50% within minutes.
MEV Maximal Extractable Value. Profit validators or miners can extract by reordering, inserting, or censoring transactions within a block they produce. MEV bots have extracted $1.5B+ from Ethereum users via sandwich attacks, arbitrage, and liquidations. Flashbots attempts to democratise and reduce harmful MEV.
